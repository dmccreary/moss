# Quiz: Moss in Architecture and Sustainability

Test your understanding of green roofs, urban design, and sustainable building practices with these review questions.

#### 1. What is the difference between extensive and intensive green roofs?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Extensive roofs use deeper substrate and require more maintenance; intensive roofs use shallow substrate
2. Extensive roofs use shallow substrate (5-15 cm) with hardy, low-maintenance plants; intensive roofs use deep substrate (15-100+ cm) with diverse plantings
3. Extensive roofs are always made of moss; intensive roofs are always made of grass
4. There is no difference; the terms are interchangeable
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Extensive green roofs use thin substrate (5-15 cm), weigh 60-150 kg/m2 when saturated, and are planted with hardy species like mosses and sedums requiring minimal maintenance. Intensive green roofs use deep substrate (15-100+ cm), weigh much more, and can support diverse plantings including shrubs and small trees but require significant ongoing maintenance. Moss is ideal for extensive roofs because it is lightweight and self-maintaining.

#### 2. Why is moss particularly well-suited as a green roof plant?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Moss grows tall enough to provide shade for rooftop equipment
2. Moss is lightweight, has no deep roots to penetrate waterproof membranes, tolerates drought, and is self-maintaining
3. Moss prevents all rainfall from reaching the roof surface
4. Moss generates enough energy to power building lighting systems
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Moss has several properties that make it ideal for green roofs: it is lightweight (important for structural load), shallow-rooted via rhizoids that will not penetrate waterproof membranes, drought-tolerant through desiccation tolerance, and self-maintaining with no mowing, fertilizing, or regular watering required. These characteristics align perfectly with the needs of extensive green roof systems.

#### 3. How does a green roof reduce the urban heat island effect?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. By reflecting more heat back into the atmosphere than a dark roof
2. By absorbing sunlight and converting it to heat more efficiently
3. Through evapotranspiration, which cools the air, and by replacing dark heat-absorbing surfaces with vegetation
4. By trapping more heat inside the building
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. Green roofs mitigate the urban heat island effect through evapotranspiration (the process of water evaporating from plant surfaces cools the surrounding air) and by replacing dark, heat-absorbing roofing materials with vegetation that reflects more light and absorbs less heat. Studies show green roofs can be 15-25 degrees C cooler than conventional dark roofs on hot days.

    **Concept Tested:** Heat Island Mitigation

#### 4. What role does moss play in stormwater management for urban buildings?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Moss channels water directly into storm drains more efficiently
2. Moss on green roofs absorbs the first several millimeters of rainfall, reducing peak runoff volume and delaying runoff timing
3. Moss waterproofs the roof surface so no water penetrates
4. Moss has no effect on stormwater management
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Moss on green roofs absorbs rainfall like a sponge, capturing the first several millimeters of any rain event. This reduces the total volume of stormwater runoff reaching storm drains and delays the timing of runoff peaks. In cities with combined sewer systems, this reduction in runoff can prevent sewage overflows during heavy rain. A moss-covered roof can retain 50-80% of annual rainfall.

    **Concept Tested:** Stormwater Management

#### 5. Which green building certification system uses a points-based rating across categories including sustainable sites, water efficiency, energy, and materials?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. The Living Building Challenge
2. Energy Star
3. LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design)
4. The Passive House Standard
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **C**. LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is the most widely used green building certification system worldwide. It awards points across categories including sustainable sites, water efficiency, energy and atmosphere, materials and resources, and indoor environmental quality. Green roofs and living walls with moss can earn LEED credits in multiple categories, including stormwater management, heat island reduction, and biodiversity.

    **Concept Tested:** LEED Certification

#### 6. How does moss contribute to a building's thermal insulation?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Moss generates heat through rapid metabolism
2. A moss layer on roofs or walls traps air, reduces heat transfer, and moderates temperature swings between interior and exterior
3. Moss reflects all infrared radiation away from the building
4. Moss only provides insulation during summer, not winter
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. A moss layer (living or in a green roof/wall system) provides thermal insulation by trapping air within its dense structure, reducing heat transfer between the building interior and exterior. This moderates temperature swings — keeping buildings cooler in summer and warmer in winter — which reduces energy consumption for both heating and cooling. The insulating effect works year-round.

    **Concept Tested:** Thermal Insulation

#### 7. What does "permeable surface" mean in the context of urban green infrastructure?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. A surface that blocks all water infiltration
2. A surface that allows water to pass through into the ground below, reducing runoff
3. A surface that is transparent to visible light
4. A surface that is made exclusively of concrete
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. A permeable surface allows water to pass through it and infiltrate into the ground below, rather than running off into storm drains. Moss-covered ground and green infrastructure create permeable surfaces that absorb rainfall, recharge groundwater, reduce flooding, and filter pollutants. This contrasts with impermeable surfaces like conventional pavement, which generate large volumes of stormwater runoff.

    **Concept Tested:** Permeable Surfaces

#### 8. A city planner wants to reduce both stormwater runoff and summer temperatures in a commercial district. Which approach addresses both goals simultaneously?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Adding more parking lots with dark asphalt
2. Installing moss-based green roofs and green infrastructure across the district
3. Removing all vegetation to increase reflective surfaces
4. Building taller buildings to create more shade
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Moss-based green roofs and green infrastructure address both stormwater and temperature concerns simultaneously. The moss absorbs rainfall (reducing runoff), while evapotranspiration and the replacement of dark surfaces with vegetation reduce temperatures (mitigating the urban heat island effect). This multi-benefit approach is central to the concept of climate-resilient cities.

    **Concept Tested:** Climate-Resilient Cities

#### 9. Why does moss provide effective sound absorption in buildings?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Moss generates white noise that masks other sounds
2. The soft, porous structure of moss and its substrate absorbs sound waves rather than reflecting them
3. Moss blocks all sound frequencies equally
4. Moss is too thin to have any effect on acoustics
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Moss and its substrate have a soft, porous structure that absorbs sound waves rather than reflecting them back into the room. This makes moss walls and green roof systems effective at reducing noise levels both inside buildings (reducing reverberation) and outside (buffering traffic and urban noise). Moss walls in offices and restaurants are increasingly valued for their acoustic benefits.

    **Concept Tested:** Sound Absorption

#### 10. Which concept describes using natural systems like moss gardens, green roofs, and bioswales instead of conventional concrete infrastructure for stormwater management?

<div class="upper-alpha" markdown>
1. Gray infrastructure
2. Green infrastructure
3. Industrial ecology
4. Structural engineering
</div>

??? question "Show Answer"
    The correct answer is **B**. Green infrastructure uses natural or nature-based systems — including green roofs, moss gardens, bioswales, rain gardens, and permeable pavements — to manage stormwater, reduce flooding, and improve water quality. It contrasts with "gray infrastructure" (conventional concrete pipes and drains). Green infrastructure is increasingly favored because it provides multiple co-benefits: stormwater management, habitat creation, carbon sequestration, and aesthetic improvement.

    **Concept Tested:** Green Infrastructure
